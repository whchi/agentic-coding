# News to Alpha Skill

## Core Idea

Do not only ask:

> Is this news important?

Ask instead:

> Is there a real demand change behind this news? Can this demand change the financial results of some small companies?

The goal of this skill is to turn news into an investment research path:

**News → Demand → Financial impact → Small-cap elasticity → Verification path**

This skill is not for making emotional trades. It is for finding real, testable alpha.

---

## 1. First Step: Is This Just News Talk, or a Demand Turning Point?

Most people stop at the story level:

- AI won a game.
- A product became popular.
- A technology had a breakthrough.
- A company started large buying.
- An app suddenly became famous.

But the first question should be:

> Is there a real and visible demand change that has already happened?

The key words are **already happened**.

Do not focus on dreams or long-term stories first. Look for real signs:

- Users are already using it.
- Companies are already buying it.
- Suppliers are already shipping it.
- Prices are already rising.
- Production schedules are already tight.
- Management teams already mention it in earnings calls.
- A small ecosystem is already becoming crowded.

Example:

The main fact about AlphaGo was not only:

> AI is strong.

The better financial meaning was:

> Deep learning showed it can use huge computing power and create superhuman results.

The main fact about a Raspberry Pi robot project is not only:

> This robot project is cool.

The better financial meaning is:

> A real open-source project is making many people buy Raspberry Pi devices.

---

## 2. Translate the Event Into Revenue

This is the most important part of the skill.

Many people stop at simple ideas:

- AI is powerful.
- Robots are hot.
- Data centers are growing.
- Electricity is tight.
- Optical modules have strong demand.

But real alpha often comes from this question:

> Who will sell more because of this? What will they sell? How much can they sell? What is the margin? Can this appear in financial reports?

Use this sentence pattern:

> Because X is happening, demand for Y is increasing.  
> This demand may go to company A, B, or C.  
> One company has a small market cap, low attention, and a low revenue base.  
> So the same demand shock may strongly change its revenue and profit.

Example 1:

Surface news:

> AlphaGo beat humans.

Financial translation:

> Deep learning needs huge computing power.  
> This increases demand for GPU, TPU, advanced chips, HBM, servers, data centers, and power.  
> Then we look for companies that can directly receive these orders.

Example 2:

Surface news:

> An open-source robot project using Raspberry Pi becomes popular.

Financial translation:

> Developers and users may buy many Raspberry Pi devices.  
> Raspberry Pi revenue may become better than expected.  
> If the company is small, the financial impact may be large.

---

## 3. Look for Shovel Sellers, But Focus on Small Shovels

In a gold rush, shovel sellers can win.

But not every shovel seller gives strong alpha.

Large companies like Microsoft, Apple, or Google may benefit. But the impact may be too small compared with their company size.

The better target is a company where:

> New demand / Current revenue or market cap is large enough.

Simple formula:

> Alpha elasticity ≈ New demand shock ÷ Company size

The same demand shock can have very different effects:

- For Microsoft, it may only add 1%.
- For a 5 billion USD company, it may add 30% revenue.
- For a 500 million USD company, it may change the whole valuation story.

So look for companies with these features:

- Small market cap
- High business purity
- Low revenue base
- Important supply position
- Not correctly understood by the market
- Financial results can show change in 1 to 4 quarters

---

## 4. Check If the Company Is Misclassified

Many alpha chances come from market misclassification.

The market may still use an old label for a company.

Example:

In 2017, many people saw TSMC as:

> A chip foundry.

But from the AI computing view, it was becoming:

> A key manufacturing gate for the AI computing race.

In 2017, many people saw Nvidia as:

> A gaming GPU company.

But it was becoming:

> A deep learning computing infrastructure company.

Many people may see Raspberry Pi as:

> An education board company.

But in a new robot ecosystem, it can become:

> A hardware entry point for open-source robots.

The key question is:

> What does the market think this company is now?  
> What is it really becoming?

If these two answers are very different, there may be alpha.

---

## 5. Build a Financial Verification Chain

Do not only tell a story. Build a way to check the story.

For every news item, ask:

> When will this logic appear in numbers?

Common verification signals:

- Revenue growth is revised upward.
- Gross margin improves.
- Backlog increases.
- Delivery lead time becomes longer.
- Inventory goes down.
- Capacity utilization rises.
- Management starts to mention this demand.
- Customer mix changes.
- ASP increases.
- Capital expenditure is revised upward.

A strong alpha idea should become a testable prediction.

For example:

> Revenue may grow 48% to 55%.

Then we compare it with the real result.

The key skill is:

> Turn the story into a financial forecast.

---

## 6. Screen for Market Cap Elasticity

Use this formula to quickly judge the possible alpha strength:

> Potential alpha strength = Demand certainty × Financial transmission clarity × Company purity × Market cap elasticity × Market neglect

Use these questions:

| Factor | Question |
|---|---|
| Demand certainty | Has the demand already happened, or is it only an idea? |
| Financial transmission | Can this demand clearly go to a company? |
| Company purity | Is the company a direct winner, not only a small side winner? |
| Market cap elasticity | Is the impact large compared with revenue, profit, or market cap? |
| Market neglect | Has the market not yet used the right label for the company? |
| Verification period | Can we see evidence in 1 to 4 quarters? |
| Downside risk | If the idea is wrong, how much can we lose? |

---

## 7. Position Rule: Use Small Positions for Testable Non-Consensus Ideas

Do not go all-in at the beginning.

A better method is:

> Start with a small position, wait for real evidence, then decide whether to add.

Early alpha ideas often have problems:

- The idea is right, but too early.
- Demand is real, but the company cannot get it.
- The company gets demand, but the market does not care.
- The market understands it, but valuation is already too high.
- It is only a short-term trend, not a lasting demand change.

Position size should match certainty.

| Situation | Position attitude |
|---|---|
| The event is real, but financial transmission is unclear | Watch or use a very small position |
| The event is real, and transmission is clear, but there is no financial proof yet | Use a small test position |
| Financial reports start to prove the idea, but the market still underestimates it | Add position |
| The market fully agrees, and valuation is high | Lower expectations or treat it as a trade |
| The logic is proved wrong | Exit |

## 8. Reverse Filter: What News Has No Alpha?

Many big news stories have little investment value.

Filter out these cases:

### 1. Only emotion, no demand

Example:

> A technology shocked the world.

But there is no new buying, no user growth, and no supply chain change.

This is only a talking point.

### 2. The demand is real, but the company is too large

Some trends may help Apple, Microsoft, or Google.

This may help direction judgment, but it may not create strong excess return.

### 3. The company benefits, but the market already priced it in

If everyone already knows the company is the main winner, the price may already include the good news.

Then look for second-order or third-order winners.

### 4. The transmission chain is too long

Example:

> AI improves human productivity, so a consumer company may benefit.

This chain is too long. There are too many variables.

### 5. There is no financial verification point

If there is no way to check the idea in financial numbers, it can become belief-based investing.

## 9. Fixed Output Template

When the user gives a news item, analyze it with this format.

### A. Surface News

Explain what the news says on the surface.

### B. Demand Change That Already Happened

Find the real demand change behind the news.

### C. Financial Translation

Explain how the demand can become revenue, cost, margin, or profit.

### D. Beneficiary Chain

List possible winners:

- First-order winners
- Second-order winners
- Third-order winners

### E. Small-Cap High-Elasticity Candidates

Find companies that may have:

- Small market cap
- High business purity
- Low revenue base
- High demand sensitivity
- Low market attention

### F. Market Misclassification

Explain:

> What does the market think this company is?  
> What may it really be becoming?

### G. Verification Indicators

List what to check in the next 1 to 4 quarters:

- Revenue
- Margin
- Backlog
- Lead time
- Inventory
- Capacity use
- Management comments
- ASP
- Customer mix
- Capital expenditure

### H. Downside Risk

Explain where the logic can be wrong.

### I. Position View

Use one of these:

- Watch
- Very small position
- Small test position
- Add after evidence
- Trade only
- Avoid
- Exit if disproved

---

## 10. Short Version

When you see news, do not ask:

> Is this big news?

Ask:

> Is there a demand change that has already happened?  
> Can this demand enter some company financial reports?  
> Is there a company small enough to be changed by this demand?  
> Is the market using the wrong label for this company?  
> Can I use a small position and wait for financial proof?

This is the core of the skill:

> Use the path of News → Demand → Financial report → Small-cap elasticity → Verification path to find market alpha.
